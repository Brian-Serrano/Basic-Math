In statistics, the exponential distribution is a continuous probability distribution that models the time between events in a Poisson process. It is often used to describe the time until the occurrence of a specific event, such as the waiting time for the next customer arrival at a service counter or the time until a radioactive particle decays.

The exponential distribution is characterized by a single parameter, λ (lambda), which is the rate parameter. The rate parameter λ represents the average number of events per unit of time. The probability density function (PDF) of the exponential distribution is given by the formula:

  • f(x) = λ * e(-λx)

where:

  • f(x) is the probability density function at a given value x,
  • λ is the rate parameter, and
  • e is the base of the natural logarithm (approximately 2.71828).

Example:

Suppose customers arrive at a service counter according to a Poisson process with an average rate of 2 customers per minute. What is the probability that the next customer arrives within 3 minutes?

Here, λ = 2 (rate parameter) and we want to calculate P(X < 3), where X follows an exponential distribution.

To calculate this probability, we use the exponential distribution formula:

  • P(X < 3) = ∫[0 to 3] λ * e(-λx) dx

The integral evaluates to:

  • P(X < 3) = ∫[0 to 3] 2 * e(-λx) dx
  • = [-e(-2x)] [0 to 3]
  • = -e(-23) + e(-20)
  • = -e(-6) + 1
  • ≈ 0.9975

So, the probability that the next customer arrives within 3 minutes is approximately 0.9975.

The exponential distribution is widely used in various applications, such as reliability analysis, queueing theory, and survival analysis. It provides a useful tool for modeling continuous random variables when events occur independently and at a constant rate over time.
