In statistics, the Poisson distribution is a discrete probability distribution that models the number of events that occur in a fixed interval of time or space. It is often used when the events happen randomly and independently, with an average rate of occurrence. The distribution is characterized by a single parameter, λ (lambda), which represents the average rate of occurrence of the events.

The probability mass function (PMF) of the Poisson distribution is given by the formula:

  • P(X = k) = e(-λ) * λkk!

where:

  • P(X = k) is the probability of exactly k events occurring in the interval,
  • e is the base of the natural logarithm (approximately 2.71828),
  • λ is the average rate of occurrence of events, and
  • k is the number of events.

Here are a couple of examples that illustrate the Poisson distribution:

Example 1:

In a manufacturing process, an average of 3 defective items are produced per hour. What is the probability of exactly 2 defective items in a randomly selected hour?

Here, λ = 3 (average rate of defective items per hour) and we want to calculate P(X = 2), where X follows a Poisson distribution.

  • P(X = 2) = e(-3) * 322!
  • = 0.0498 * 92
  • ≈ 0.1494

So, the probability of exactly 2 defective items in an hour is approximately 0.1494.

Example 2:

On average, a call center receives 6 customer complaints per day. What is the probability of receiving more than 10 customer complaints in a randomly selected day?

Here, λ = 6 (average rate of customer complaints per day) and we want to calculate P(X > 10).

P(X > 10) = 1 - P(X ≤ 10)

= 1 - [P(X = 0) + P(X = 1) + ... + P(X = 10)]

You can compute the probabilities for each value using the Poisson formula and subtract them from 1 to find the result.

The Poisson distribution is commonly used in various applications, such as modeling rare events, analyzing queues and waiting times, and predicting event occurrences in areas like telecommunications and insurance.
