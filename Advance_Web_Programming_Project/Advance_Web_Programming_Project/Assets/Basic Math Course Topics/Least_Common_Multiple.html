In mathematics, the least common multiple (LCM) of two or more numbers is the smallest positive integer that is divisible by all of the given numbers without leaving a remainder. Finding the LCM is useful in various mathematical operations, such as adding or subtracting fractions, solving equations, and working with ratios. Let's discuss how to find the LCM and provide examples:

To find the LCM of two or more numbers, you can use either the prime factorization method or the method of listing multiples. Let's go through both methods:

  1. Prime Factorization Method:

    To find the LCM using the prime factorization method, follow these steps:

    • Prime factorize each number into its prime factors.
    • Identify the highest power of each prime factor that appears in any of the numbers.
    • Multiply all the prime factors, each raised to its highest power.

    Example:

    Let's find the LCM of 12 and 18 using the prime factorization method:

    • Prime factorization of 12: 22 * 31
    • Prime factorization of 18: 21 * 32
    • To find the LCM, we take the highest power of each prime factor:
    • LCM = 22 * 32 = 4 * 9 = 36

    So, the LCM of 12 and 18 is 36.

  2. Method of Listing Multiples:

    To find the LCM using the method of listing multiples, follow these steps:

    • List the multiples of each number until you find a common multiple.
    • The smallest common multiple is the LCM.

    Example:

    Let's find the LCM of 8 and 15 using the method of listing multiples:

    • Multiples of 8: 8, 16, 24, 32, 40, ...
    • Multiples of 15: 15, 30, 45, 60, ...
    • The smallest common multiple is 120.

    So, the LCM of 8 and 15 is 120.

The LCM is used in various mathematical contexts, such as simplifying fractions, finding a common denominator, or solving equations involving multiple variables. It helps ensure that different numbers align properly and can be combined or compared effectively.
