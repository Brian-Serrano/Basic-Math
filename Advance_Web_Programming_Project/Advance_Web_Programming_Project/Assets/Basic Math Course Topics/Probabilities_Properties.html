Here are some basic properties of probabilities:

  1. Probability Range: The probability of any event E is a number between 0 and 1, inclusive. A probability of 0 indicates that the event is impossible, while a probability of 1 means the event is certain to occur.

    Example: The probability of rolling a number greater than 7 on a standard six-sided die is 0 because it is impossible.

  2. Complement Rule: The probability of the complement of an event E (denoted as E') is equal to 1 minus the probability of E.

    Example: If the probability of event A is 0.6, then the probability of event A not occurring (A') is 1 - 0.6 = 0.4.

  3. Addition Rule: For any two events A and B, the probability of either event A or event B occurring (denoted as A or B) is the sum of their individual probabilities, minus the probability of both events occurring simultaneously (denoted as A and B).

    Example: Consider rolling a fair six-sided die. The probability of rolling an even number (event A) is 36 = 0.5, and the probability of rolling a number less than 4 (event B) is 36 = 0.5. The probability of rolling either an even number or a number less than 4 is 0.5 + 0.5 - 16 = 56.

  4. Multiplication Rule for Independent Events: For any two independent events A and B, the probability of both events occurring (denoted as A and B) is the product of their individual probabilities.

    Example: Consider flipping a fair coin twice. The probability of getting heads on the first flip (event A) is 12, and the probability of getting heads on the second flip (event B) is also 12. The probability of getting heads on both flips is 12 * 12 = 14.

  5. Multiplication Rule for Dependent Events: For two dependent events A and B, the probability of both events occurring (denoted as A and B) is the product of the probability of event A and the conditional probability of event B given that A has already occurred.

    Example: Consider drawing a card from a standard deck without replacement. The probability of drawing a red card on the first draw (event A) is 2652 = 12. If a red card is drawn on the first draw, the probability of drawing another red card on the second draw (event B) is 2551. The probability of drawing two red cards in a row is 12 * 2551 = 25102.

These properties help us understand and calculate probabilities in various situations. They are fundamental in probability theory and provide a basis for more advanced concepts and calculations.
