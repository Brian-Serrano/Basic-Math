In statistics, the normal distribution, also known as the Gaussian distribution or the bell curve, is a continuous probability distribution that is widely used to model a wide range of natural phenomena. It is symmetric and bell-shaped, with its shape determined by two parameters: the mean (μ) and the standard deviation (σ). The mean represents the center of the distribution, while the standard deviation controls the spread or variability of the data.

The probability density function (PDF) of the normal distribution is given by the formula:

  • f(x) = 1σ * √(2π) * e-(x - μ)22σ2

where:

  • f(x) is the probability density function at a given value x,
  • μ is the mean of the distribution,
  • σ is the standard deviation of the distribution,
  • π is a mathematical constant (approximately 3.14159), and
  • e is the base of the natural logarithm (approximately 2.71828).

Here are a couple of examples that illustrate the normal distribution:

Example 1:

The heights of adult males in a population are normally distributed with a mean of 175 cm and a standard deviation of 6 cm. What is the probability of a randomly selected adult male being between 170 cm and 180 cm tall?

Here, μ = 175 cm (mean) and σ = 6 cm (standard deviation). We want to calculate P(170 ≤ X ≤ 180), where X follows a normal distribution.

To calculate this probability, we need to find the area under the normal curve between 170 and 180. This can be done using statistical tables or software.

Example 2:

The scores on a standardized test are normally distributed with a mean of 80 and a standard deviation of 10. What is the probability of scoring above 90 on the test?

Here, μ = 80 (mean) and σ = 10 (standard deviation). We want to calculate P(X > 90), where X follows a normal distribution.

To calculate this probability, we need to find the area under the normal curve to the right of 90. Again, this can be done using statistical tables or software.

The normal distribution is widely used in various fields of statistics, such as hypothesis testing, confidence intervals, and data analysis. It provides a convenient and powerful tool for modeling continuous random variables and making statistical inferences.
